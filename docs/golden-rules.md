# Golden Rules — the DirtBikeX content framework and quality bar

The single source of truth for what makes a DirtBikeX video good. Every topic, cover,
script, shot, subtitle, and edit is judged against this document; the skill files
operationalize it stage by stage. It applies to both language variants and every
platform. When a rule here conflicts with convenience, the rule wins; when it genuinely
conflicts with a platform norm, record the exception in the episode's `edit-notes.md`.

(The original source document is preserved verbatim at `docs/archive/FRAMEWORK.md`.)

## The core principle

Short videos are not a tool for self-expression. They are a tool for **designing viewer
reactions**. A DirtBikeX video does not express what we want to say — it solves a
concrete problem for a rider, track owner, brand, or community member, and every line of
script, every shot, every subtitle, and every visual element serves this chain:

```text
留下来 retention → 看得懂 understanding → 共鸣 resonance → 收藏 saving → 评论 commenting → 关注 following
```

If you cannot name which reaction a segment is designed to trigger, cut the segment.

**The judgment test.** A script is not mature until it answers all four:

> Why would the viewer **like** this video?
> Why would they **save** it?
> Why would they **comment**?
> Why would they **follow** us?

## The five design dimensions

Score every topic, script, and cut against all five:

| Dimension | 中文 | The question it answers |
|-----------|------|------------------------|
| User relevance | 用户相关 | Whose concrete problem does this solve? Name the person. |
| Emotional trigger | 情绪触发 | What does the viewer feel in the first 5 seconds — pain, surprise, desire, indignation? |
| Information density | 信息密度 | Does every sentence move the viewer forward? Zero filler. |
| Personal positioning | 人设定位 | Why us — what riding/track/community experience backs this up? |
| Interaction conversion | 互动转化 | What is the viewer explicitly given to save, comment on, or follow for? |

A topic that scores weak on two or more dimensions is not shot. Fix the angle or drop it.

## Topic (选题)

Do not start from our knowledge inventory ("I recently learned something, so I want to
share it"). Start from the viewer's stuck point and reverse-engineer:

> What problem is the target viewer stuck on right now?
> Do we have experience, a case, a tool, or a method that solves it?
> Can it be compressed into one video?

**Topic formula: user pain point + our solution + concrete benefit.**

> Example: many beginner riders don't know how to choose their first dirt bike →
> "the no-regret first-bike checklist".

- One episode = one problem = one core conclusion. Split anything bigger.
- The viewer benefit must be stateable in one sentence before scripting starts.
- A viral video is not merely useful — it makes the viewer want to interact. Design all
  four interaction triggers deliberately:

| Trigger | Design it by |
|---------|--------------|
| "This is about me" | Naming the exact problem the viewer is living |
| Emotional reaction | Anxiety, resonance, contrast, surprise, being understood |
| Save-worthy value | Checklist, steps, formula, pitfall list |
| Comment motivation | Controversy, choice, experience sharing, debate |

## Packaging (封面/标题)

- Title, cover, and first frame are one promise, stated three ways. They must agree.
- Titles are built on the four opening formulas below, same as hooks.
- The cover is a packaging **hypothesis** written before the script, but locked only
  after the edit — the best first frame often comes from real footage.
- No clickbait the video doesn't cash: the hook promise must be paid off inside the video.

## Hook (开头 2 秒)

The first 2 seconds must land one of the four opening formulas. Never open with
self-introduction, logos, channel branding, or "today we're going to…". The first
subtitle line and the first spoken line are written as one unit.

**A. Pain point first** — hit the hidden frustration immediately.
> You think beginner riders crash because their skills are bad. But often, the first
> mistake was choosing the wrong bike.

**B. Identity contrast** — use conflict to create retention.
> I rode dirt bikes for three months before realizing most beginners choose their first
> bike completely backward.

**C. Benefit promise** — say exactly what the viewer will gain.
> This video will help you avoid the five biggest mistakes beginners make when buying
> their first dirt bike.

**D. Counterintuitive claim** — break a common assumption.
> For your first dirt bike, I really do not recommend starting with the most powerful one.

## Body (正文)

**Every sentence moves information forward and is one of six functional types.** A
sentence that is none of them gets deleted:

| Sentence type | Function |
|---------------|----------|
| Pain point | Makes the viewer feel "this is about me" |
| Conclusion | Gives a direct judgment |
| Reversal | Breaks an existing assumption |
| Value | Gives method, step, standard, or framework |
| Case | Uses a concrete story to build credibility |
| Action | Guides saving, commenting, following, or execution |

**Filler blacklist** — these (and their zh-CN equivalents) never survive a draft:

> "Today let's talk about…" · "Many people may not know…" · "Next I'll share with
> you…" · "This is actually very important…" · "Personally, I think…"

Instead of filler, state the judgment:

> The biggest thing beginners overlook is not power. It is seat height, bike weight,
> and maintenance cost.

**Positioning is embedded, never introduced.** Compress experience into a useful signal;
the background supports the content, never interrupts it.

> ✗ "Hi, I'm XXX. I like dirt bikes and have done a lot of research."
> ✓ "From being a complete beginner to finishing my first full trail loop, most of my
>   mistakes came from these three things." (journey compressed into one line)
> ✓ "I bought the wrong first bike myself, so this video only covers the mistakes
>   beginners are most likely to regret." (content scoped by a past mistake)

**Weaken the self, strengthen the viewer benefit.** Not "what happened to me" but "what
my experience helps you avoid":

> I crashed three times on my first trail ride. I'm not saying this to tell you how
> miserable I was — I'm saying it because beginners should not practice speed first.
> They should practice braking and standing posture first.

**Structure beats improvisation.** Required script sections, in order (template:
`templates/episode/script.template.md`, workflow: `skills/03-script-writing.md`):

```text
1. Hook            [pain point / contrast / benefit / counterintuitive]
2. User problem    the surface problem vs the root problem
3. Core conclusion one-sentence judgment, stated early
4. Key points      usually three: judgment + reason + example each
5. Personal experience  the mistake we made, and the lesson — proof, not autobiography
6. Checklist       the saveable artifact
7. Follow expectation   what we keep solving next
```

## Shots (镜头)

Every shot carries an information function — shots that only look good are cut
candidates. Default design per script section:

| Script section | Shot design |
|----------------|-------------|
| Hook | Close-up, direct eye contact, large subtitle carrying the pain/contrast |
| Problem | Fast cuts of the mistake, user scenarios, comment screenshots |
| Key points / method | Medium shot + keyword subtitles + simple visual diagram |
| Experience / proof | Real personal footage, photos, data, screenshots |
| Checklist | Card layout, list-style subtitles, numbered steps |
| Follow reason | Return to face camera; reinforce what we keep solving |

## Subtitles (字幕)

The division of labor is fixed:

> **Voiceover explains. Subtitles highlight. Visuals reinforce emotion and memory.**

- Subtitles never merely transcribe. Compress each passage into its keywords — key
  numbers, verbs, and conclusions get emphasis; connective tissue is dropped.

  > VO: "The biggest mistake beginners make when choosing their first dirt bike is not
  > engine displacement. It is whether they can actually control the bike."
  > Subtitle: "Beginner bike choice: don't look at displacement first —
  > seat height / weight / control"

- Keep density readable: zh-CN ≤ ~15 characters per line, en-US ≤ ~38 characters per
  line, one to two lines max on 9:16.
- Subtitles must survive platform UI: keep them inside the safe zone
  (see [platforms.md](platforms.md)).

## Ending (结尾)

A follow is not "this was useful" — it is the viewer thinking *"this person will keep
solving problems I care about."* End by creating that future expectation:

> ✗ "If you liked this, follow me."
> ✓ "I'll keep breaking down the most common beginner dirt-bike mistakes. Next video:
>   how to choose your first set of protective gear." (next-video teaser)
> ✓ "If you're preparing to buy your first bike, save this video first. I'll keep
>   breaking down beginner bike choice, gear, riding locations, and training routes."
>   (save-first CTA + series roadmap)

- The CTA asks for **one** action, matched to the content: save for checklists, comment
  for opinions/questions, follow for series continuity.

## Localization bar

- zh-CN and en-US are sibling variants: same core message, beats, and visual system;
  independently written hooks, examples, idioms, pacing, and captions.
- A variant that reads like a translation fails review. See [localization.md](localization.md).

## The ten-question content gate (pre-publish)

Answered in the publishing checklist before any upload:

1. Do the first two seconds directly hit a user pain point?
2. Does the target viewer immediately feel "this is about me"?
3. Is there a clear benefit promise?
4. Is there contrast, counterintuitive insight, or emotional fluctuation?
5. Does every sentence move the information forward?
6. Is there something worth saving — checklist, step-by-step method, formula?
7. Is there a reason to comment — question, debate, shared experience?
8. Is our own experience embedded to build credibility?
9. Does the ending create future expectation?
10. What is this video's primary goal — likes, saves, comments, shares, or follows —
    and is the content actually designed for it?

## Hard rules (non-negotiable)

1. No video ships without a hook that lands in ≤ 2 seconds.
2. No self-introduction openings, ever.
3. No filler sentences — information density is the house style.
4. Subtitles emphasize; they never just repeat.
5. Every video names its viewer and pays off its promise.
6. Every video ends with a designed follow expectation.
7. Every published video gets a `review.md` with real metrics before the next episode of
   that series is scripted.
8. No video is uploaded before the ten-question content gate above is answered in the
   publishing checklist.
