You can distill this video into a reusable **viral short-video scripting framework**.

The core idea is not “what do I want to say?” but:

**user relevance → emotional trigger → information density → personal positioning → interaction conversion**

This can be directly inserted into your video workflow.

---

## 1. Topic Selection: Shift from “What I Want to Say” to “What the User Wants to Hear”

Do not start from your own knowledge inventory. Start from the user’s pain point and reverse-engineer the content.

**Wrong logic:**

> I recently learned something, so I want to share it.

**Correct logic:**

> What problem is my target audience currently stuck on?
> Do I have experience, a case, a book, a tool, or a method that can solve it?
> Can I compress it into one video?

Reusable topic formula:

> User pain point + my solution + concrete benefit

Example:

> Many beginner riders do not know how to choose their first dirt bike, so I made a “no-regret first bike checklist.”

---

## 2. The Nature of Viral Content: It Is Not Just “Good Content,” but Strong Interaction Motivation

A viral video is not merely useful. It makes the viewer want to interact.

You should deliberately design four types of interaction psychology:

| Interaction Trigger | How to Design It                                         |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| “This is about me”  | Directly name the problem the user is experiencing       |
| Emotional reaction  | Anxiety, resonance, contrast, surprise, being understood |
| Save-worthy value   | Checklist, steps, formula, pitfall list                  |
| Comment motivation  | Controversy, choice, experience sharing, debate          |

A reusable judgment test:

> Why would the user like this video?
> Why would they save it?
> Why would they comment?
> Why would they follow me?

If you cannot answer these, the script is not mature enough.

---

## 3. First Two Seconds: Hook First, Do Not Introduce Yourself First

Do not start with:

> Hi everyone, I’m XXX. Today I want to share...

Go straight into the user’s pain point, contrast, benefit, or counterintuitive claim.

Four opening formulas:

### A. Pain Point First

Hit the user’s hidden frustration immediately.

> You think beginner riders crash because their skills are bad. But often, the first mistake was choosing the wrong bike.

### B. Identity Contrast

Use conflict to create retention.

> I rode dirt bikes for three months before realizing most beginners choose their first bike completely backward.

### C. Benefit Promise

Tell the viewer exactly what they will gain.

> This video will help you avoid the five biggest mistakes beginners make when buying their first dirt bike.

### D. Counterintuitive Claim

Say something that goes against common assumptions.

> For your first dirt bike, I really do not recommend starting with the most powerful one.

---

## 4. Script Structure: Every Sentence Must Move the Information Forward

Oral scripts should not contain filler. Every sentence should serve a function.

High-density scripts usually contain only these types of sentences:

| Sentence Type       | Function                                           |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Pain point sentence | Makes the user feel “this is about me”             |
| Conclusion sentence | Gives a direct judgment                            |
| Reversal sentence   | Breaks the user’s existing assumption              |
| Value sentence      | Gives method, step, standard, or framework         |
| Case sentence       | Uses a concrete story to build credibility         |
| Action sentence     | Guides saving, commenting, following, or execution |

Remove filler like:

> Today let’s talk about...
> Many people may not know...
> Next I’ll share with you...
> This is actually very important...
> Personally, I think...

Better expression:

> The biggest thing beginners overlook is not power. It is seat height, bike weight, and maintenance cost.

---

## 5. Self-Introduction Principle: Do Not Introduce Your Identity; Embed Your Positioning

Mature creators do not avoid talking about themselves. They compress their experience into useful positioning signals.

Do not say:

> Hi, I’m XXX. I like dirt bikes and have done a lot of research.

Say:

> From being a complete beginner to finishing my first full trail loop, most of my mistakes came from these three things.

Or:

> I bought the wrong first bike myself, so this video only covers the mistakes beginners are most likely to regret.

Your personal background should support the content, not interrupt it.

---

## 6. Content Design: Weaken the Self, Strengthen User Benefit

Every video should start from user benefit, not creator self-expression.

Reusable expression structure:

> Not “what happened to me,” but “what my experience can help you avoid.”

Example:

> I crashed three times on my first trail ride. I’m not saying this to tell you how miserable I was. I’m saying it because beginners should not practice speed first. They should practice braking and standing posture first.

---

## 7. Shot Design: Every Shot Should Serve Information Progression

Although the original video is mainly about oral scripting, the same logic applies to camera work:

**Each shot should correspond to an information function.**

Suggested shot structure:

| Script Section       | Shot Design                                                                        |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Opening hook         | Close-up, direct eye contact, large subtitle highlighting pain point or contrast   |
| Pain point expansion | Fast cuts of common mistakes, user scenarios, comment screenshots                  |
| Method explanation   | Medium shot + keyword subtitles + simple visual diagram                            |
| Proof / case         | Insert real personal experience, backend data, riding footage, screenshots         |
| Summary checklist    | List-style subtitles, card layout, numbered steps                                  |
| Follow reason        | Return to face camera and reinforce what future problems you will continue solving |

Shots should not just look good. They should carry information.

---

## 8. Subtitle and On-Screen Copy: Do Not Repeat the Script; Extract the Key Points

Subtitles should not simply duplicate every spoken word. They should compress each section into keywords.

For example, if the voiceover says:

> The biggest mistake beginners make when choosing their first dirt bike is not engine displacement. It is whether they can actually control the bike.

The on-screen subtitle can be:

> Beginner bike choice: do not look at displacement first
> Look at: seat height / weight / controllability

Subtitle principle:

> Voiceover explains.
> Subtitles highlight.
> Visuals reinforce emotion and memory.

---

## 9. Ending Design: Do Not Just Say “Follow Me”; Create Future Expectation

The essence of a follow is not just “this content was useful.” It is the user thinking:

> This person will continue solving problems I care about.

Weak ending:

> If you liked this, follow me.

Stronger ending:

> I’ll keep breaking down the most common mistakes beginner dirt bike riders make. In the next video, I’ll explain how to choose your first set of protective gear.

Or:

> If you’re preparing to buy your first bike, save this video first. I’ll continue breaking down beginner bike choice, gear, riding locations, and training routes.

---

## 10. Reusable Short-Video Script Template

Use this structure for each video:

```text
1. Opening hook:
[Pain point / contrast / benefit / counterintuitive claim]

2. User problem:
Many people are stuck on [specific problem], but the real issue is not [surface problem]. It is [root problem].

3. Core conclusion:
My recommendation is: [one-sentence judgment].

4. Three key points:
First, [judgment + reason + example].
Second, [judgment + reason + example].
Third, [judgment + reason + example].

5. Personal experience insertion:
I also made the mistake of [specific mistake], and later realized [lesson learned].

6. Summary checklist:
So just remember these points: [1] [2] [3].

7. Follow expectation:
I’ll continue covering [related content series]. If this is relevant to you, follow for the next one.
```

---

## 11. Pre-Publishing Checklist

Before publishing any video, check these ten questions:

1. Does the first two seconds directly hit a user pain point?
2. Can the target viewer immediately feel “this is about me”?
3. Is there a clear benefit promise?
4. Is there contrast, counterintuitive insight, or emotional fluctuation?
5. Does every sentence move the information forward?
6. Is there something worth saving, such as a checklist, step-by-step method, or formula?
7. Is there a reason for viewers to comment, such as a question, debate, or shared experience?
8. Is your own experience embedded to build credibility?
9. Does the ending create future expectation?
10. Is the goal of this video likes, saves, comments, shares, or follower conversion?

---

## 12. The Core Principle

The most transferable method from this video is:

> Do not treat short videos as a tool for self-expression.
> Treat them as a tool for designing user reactions.

Every line of script, every shot, every subtitle, and every visual element should serve:

**retention, understanding, resonance, saving, commenting, and following.**

For DirtBikeX, this is especially useful. Your videos should not simply say:

> I want to introduce DirtBikeX.

They should start from:

> What pain point does a beginner rider, track owner, rider group, or brand currently have?
> Can DirtBikeX or my experience solve it?
> Can this video make them feel: “This is exactly what I need”?
